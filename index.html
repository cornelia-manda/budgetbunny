BUDGETBUNNY

UI/UX Designer / Researcher / Interaction Designer

A financial companion for students, designed to demystify OSAP, track hidden costs, and promote financial literacy without the guilt of a traditional calculator.

8 Page Paper: The Problem

Our team of 3 began with an extensive eight page research phase to understand the systematic neglect of financial literacy in education. We conducted interviews with students across various years of study, including international and first generation students.

Key findings revealed that students felt lost and stressed when trying to align OSAP payments with tuition deadlines. Every participant struggled to understand where their money was going, often only reviewing bank statements after a full year of overspending.

Target Personas

Through our collaborative effort, our team developed three primary personas to guide our design decisions. These personas ensured we addressed the specific pain points identified in our interviews.

❮ ❯

Design Process

Our team of 3 followed a rigorous double diamond framework. We successfully fostered a healthy and efficient team environment and attitudes where everyone was comfortable sharing thoughts and opinions. Through productive in person work sessions, we effectively delegated tasks and consistently sought one another's opinions to ensure project cohesion.

This collaborative culture allowed us to seamlessly transition from initial paper sketches and functional requirement mapping to a robust high fidelity Figma prototype showcasing end to end interactions.

❮ ❯

Visual Identity

Our team utilized a playful identity to make financial management feel less intimidating. The interface uses a consistent mint green palette to reflect growth and financial health.

  • Typography: Kantumruy Pro (Sans-Serif)
  • Palette: Mint Green (#60E4B2) and Radiant Yellow (#F7D368)

Usability & Testing

Following the creation of our high-fidelity prototype, our team of 3 conducted five formal usability tests. Using the 10 question System Usability Likert Scale, we evaluated the efficiency of the end to end interaction and verified the clarity of our design decisions.

Key improvements prompted by the testing included clarifying navigational labels to reduce cognitive load and increasing button contrast for accessibility. These iterations ensured the final prototype represented students real world financial needs without the stress of scattered deadlines.
